# Activity 1: ERP Architecture Design — Manufacturing Case Study (PPG) 🏭🏢

## 1. Project Summary
As part of my academic coursework in **Enterprise Systems Design and Modeling (SECJ3744)**, our team acted as enterprise consultants to design a comprehensive Enterprise Resource Planning (ERP) architecture blueprint tailored to the complex global operations of **PPG Industrial** (Manufacturing Sector). The objective of this activity was to map out how a massive manufacturing entity can move away from siloed on-premises legacy applications and transition into a modern, integrated enterprise framework that aligns cross-departmental data flows seamlessly.

Our blueprint defines the system architecture using a multi-layered design and clear operational lifecycles:
* **Three-Tier Architecture Mapping:** We structured the platform layout into three distinct layers to ensure system decoupling: the **Presentation Layer** handles front-end user login and dashboard access; the **Business Layer** executes core logic calculations, transactions, and business rules; and the **Database Layer** manages back-end relational data storage.
* **Cross-Departmental Data Integration:** The architecture connects four core pillars of PPG’s workflow to eliminate data gaps: **Production** shares manufacturing timelines and material requests; **Inventory** updates live stock levels and raw material availability; **HR** coordinates schedules and employee attendance; and **Finance** reconciles expense flows and payment streams.
* **Implementation & Maintenance Lifecycles:** We focused heavily on **Phase 4 (Testing)** to mandate detailed unit, integration, and full system testing, along with **Phase 6 (Maintenance)** to support regular software updates, user feedback loops, and continuous data validation.
* **Risk & Benefit Appraisals:** While the system maximizes productivity through centralized, highly secure data profiles, it actively addresses common deployment blockers—such as complex data migration friction and fear of massive capital expenses—by advocating for scalable Cloud ERP delivery models.

## 2. Reflection

**Key Takeaway:**

* **System Separation Principles:** I learned the value of breaking an enterprise platform down into decoupled Presentation, Business, and Database layers. This assignment showed me that separating the front-end user interfaces from complex manufacturing calculations is essential to ensure a global system can update individual parts without risking entire database crashes.
* **Cross-Functional Data Dependencies:** I gained a clear understanding of how manufacturing operations rely on real-time data handshakes between separate business departments. Seeing how an entry in the production department immediately shifts inventory requirements, triggers payroll timelines in HR, and alerts accounting in Finance showed me that an ERP acts as the central brain of an enterprise.
* **Proactive Risk and Life Cycle Planning:** This activity taught me that the technical launch of a system is only a small part of enterprise engineering. Learning about testing strategies, maintenance update cycles, and cloud migration frameworks showed me that managing human resistance to change and mapping complex data integration flows early on is what prevents massive budget overruns.
